Obsolete Kana

yyyy-mm-dd

Certain kana visible on the common kana charts are not in use and turned obsolete by other kanas. ヲ comes in minde. を has also lost its unique sound and more or less sounds interchangeably like お. Some have even fallen so out of favour that they are not even on the charts anymore. like ゐ, ヱ and ゑ. These are interesting to maybe explore further but in the grand scheme of things meaningless if your goal is to communicate with modern Japanese speakers.

Listening, speaking, reading and hand-writing. Natural stuff in any study right? Obviously, obvious steps in processing a language. But what about typing on a computer keyboard? Even typing out that small and basic Japanese paragraph in only hiragana and katakana took much longer than I expected. There's so many tiny things that stops you dead in your tracks like: How do I jump between keyboard layouts and alphabets? How do I type out びっくり instead of びつくり? How do I type out characters like ?, Å, Ä, and Ö when on the Japanese keyboard layout? Why is every single keyboard shortcut not working? Not just strictly computer related writing questions like: Why is ウォ preferred over ヲ? Why has ウェcompletely erased the notion of using ヱ? Is there any difference between ぢ and じ?

Typing basic Japanese with these questions firing in every single neuron is making me feel like a new born baby. I'm now a senior citizen that types with a careful fully outstretched index finger while holding an imaginary magnifying glass in the other hand. It's back to basics. All of that crusty gray brain matter need to pave new paths. However don't fret Joshua, here's the deal:

No sane Japanese typer uses the Ctrl - Capslock, Shift + Capslock and Alt + Capslock shortcuts to jump between the alphabets. Everything is always typed out in hiragana. When you want a word to be in katakana just type it out fully in hiragana and then press F7. Actually more often than not the IME suggestion box will just give it to you in katakana.

When you start using kanji the enormous suggestion box becomes your lifesaver. That's where all the kanji suggestions are and will hopefully down the line fool people on the internet that you're not in the first grade. But it's not useful until you learn what the kanji means. There are plenty of sentences with the exact same hiragana that mean vastly different things dependant on its kanji.

Want to mix languages like you're doing right now on this post? Well, the problem is the special characters but you can find must of them just mapped out on different keys like question mark being on the hyphen key. Swedish Å, Ä and Ö though, tough luck, either customize your keyboard somehow or just get used to the Windows key + Spacebar shortcut to jump to your Swedish layout. All this is on windows 10, using the Microsoft IME and with a Swedish keyboard mind you, from my understanding these things are so fickle that it can vary dependant on whether you ate granola or oatmeal that morning. The most frequent shortcuts: Ctrl + S, Ctrl + Z and Ctrl + F actually do work. You just need to first jump the IME suggestion box first.

In order to type the important にゃー and double consonants you need to learn how to type small kanas. This is done by prefixing a kana with x and for double consonant you logically double tap the consonant. The aforementioned IME suggestion box usually got you covered though.

An oversimplified answer to the nowadays obsolete katakana in modern Japanese like ヲ and ヱ can essentially be summed up to modern speaking patterns having rid of the existing miniscule vocal difference they had. These katakana been consumed by an already existing kana. There's much more to it but meaningless to dwell over at my current stage.

Jisho Intimidation

yyyy-mm-dd

Looking up kanji in dictionaries it is easy to say something similar to "How are there so many meaning?" and "How am I supposed to remember this?" But have some perspective. I need to remind myself that this is not a Japanese specific thing. Spets. A common Swedish word. If I look up this simple word in a Swedish dictionary it hands me back 5 different definitions. Now someone completely ignorant to the Swedish language might just say the same sentences I said about Japanese. This is just how languages work. It's not specific to Japanese. And it is easy to forget about all the technically correct definitions words have that you use all the time.
